Methodology

Epistemological Stance

This study engages with an underdeveloped body of literature on the Turkish hazelnut cluster, particularly with respect to the relational dynamics among actor groups. To capture the complexities of these dynamics, the research needed an approach that offers exploratory capabilities. This led to seeking primary sources that can surface the relational attributes of the actors. Consequently, the research is philosophically underpinned by interpretivism and abductive reasoning. The former is essential to understand how actors within the cluster construct meaning and interpret their changing environments. The latter allows for a dialogue between field observations, theoretical frameworks, and the existing body of scholarship.

Historical-Interpretative Orientation

This thesis contributes to local hazelnut studies by aligning its methodology with the debates outlined in the literature. It adopts a bottom-up approach in analysing the dynamics of the hazelnut cluster, incorporating an ethnographic methodology. By using an ethnographic approach, the research provides the local dynamics that the Turkish literature lacks in addressing. While the main contribution aims to explore the strategies employed by the intermediaries during economic transition, it also makes a historiographical contribution to the history of the Turkish hazelnut cluster.

Relational Case Logic & Polyphonic Perspective

The thesis focuses on the merchant class during the transformation of the Turkish hazelnut cluster. The primary reason is that merchants occupy an intermediary position within a network that involves producers, exporters and MNCs. Merchants' central position within these networks would not be fully captured by interviewing only the merchants. For this reason, the study adopts a polyphonic design — interviewing all the actors so that it can offer a better understanding of their relationships. Actors located at different positions within the value chain were interviewed, enabling the triangulation of perspectives on the merchants' roles.

Analytical Framework: Gioia Methodology

The study uses the Gioia methodology as its main analytical strategy. The method offers a systematic way to analyse interview-based and ethnographic material while preserving participants' own categories of meaning in the early stages of coding. At the same time, it allows the analysis to move toward more abstract analytical categories in later stages. This fits the interpretive and abductive design of the thesis which examines how different actors narrate and make sense of historical and institutional change in the Turkish hazelnut cluster.

Why Gioia?

The Gioia methodology treats interviewees as "knowledgeable agents" who construct their organisational realities. In the context of this study, informants are treated as the holders of full knowledge of daily practices, market dynamics, and the cluster network — a perspective scarce in the existing literature.

Method

Research Design

The research is based on semi-structured interviews organised through a polyphonic design, conducted with actor groups occupying distinct positions within the hazelnut cluster. Interview protocols were tailored to each group considering their position within the value chain. These groups were: farmers, merchants, exporters, and relevant governance bodies.

The interview protocol uses an oral history approach to encourage participants to reflect on longer-term experiences of change, including family trajectories, entry into the sector, shifts in occupational roles, and recollections of earlier institutional arrangements. This was appropriate as the study is concerned not merely with present-day reflections, but with how historical transformations within the cluster are remembered, narrated and interpreted by differently positioned actors.

Besides being tailored to each actor group, the interview protocols share a common core addressing three broad themes: the historical evolution of the cluster, present-day market practices and institutional arrangements, and relations with other actors in the value chain. Snowball sampling was the primary data collection method, using a small pool of initial contacts to recruit further participants through their interconnected social and professional ties.

Sources

Ethnographic Fieldwork

22 interviews were conducted in August 2025 across the Black Sea Region of Turkey. August marks the hazelnut harvest season — a period in which multiple actors could be accessed in the same local setting. This facilitated snowball sampling and strengthened the ethnographic dimension by allowing observation of the cluster during a period of heightened activity.

Interviews with state or quasi-state institutions and exporters were pre-arranged through formal contact channels. Conversely, interviews with farmers, merchants, and local notable figures were spontaneous, reached through snowball referrals during fieldwork. These yielded less rehearsed, more candid accounts.

Official Documents

Data on the official history of the cluster was drawn from the TRK (Turkish Competition Authority). The primary source is the Fındık Sektörü Raporu (Hazelnut Sector Report), published in 2018 by the III. Supervision & Enforcement Department. At 118 pages, it covers market structure, supply chain dynamics, legal and competition details, structural issues, and statistical tables on efficiency, exports, and production costs.

Archival Sources

Archival documents were retrieved from the Türkiye İş Bankası archives. The total set is composed of six pieces dating from 1932 to 1938, including correspondences between the Bank's headquarters and Osmanlı Bankası, Mısır İş Limited, and the Giresun branch, as well as a declaration of the establishment of İş Bank Limited Şirketi. These provide a historical baseline against which later official and field-based narratives can be compared.

Sampling & Participants

Participants were reached through three "seed" contacts — people from the researcher's own network in Samsun and Ordu provinces. From these seeds, access was gained to local notables like muhtars (elected village representatives) and shop owners, who in turn introduced the core interviewees: exporters, merchants, and producers.

On Limitations

The sample was imbalanced in certain respects: few female respondents, limited exporter representation (only one), and concentration within specific referral circles. The majority of producer interviews came from villages in the Fatsa district of Ordu province. That said, producers in Fatsa are responsible for one-tenth of the global hazelnut supply. The study does not aim at statistical generalisation but at analytic generalisation to broader debates on intermediaries and cluster transformation.

Data Collection

The interview material consists of 22 semi-structured interviews. Twelve were audio-recorded, producing 9.55 hours of material. One recorded interview was discarded due to withdrawal of consent, leaving 11 transcripts for coding. The remaining unrecorded interviews were documented through detailed field notes.

Interview questions focused on three dimensions: actors' entry into the sector and recollections of past arrangements (historical evolution); present-day practices, pricing mechanisms, and labour relations (current state); and perceptions of relationships with other participants in the cluster (relational dynamics — trust, dependency, negotiation, cooperation, and conflict).

Use of Software & AI

Google Gemini was used to generate initial text transcriptions of audio material. All outputs were checked for reliability, and parts where AI could not transcribe were done manually. Transcriptions were verified for the integrity of literal language — a vital component of Gioia-style first-order analysis. The material was then uploaded to MAXQDA for coding, categorisation, and generation of code-related charts.

Ethical Approval

The study was conducted in accordance with GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) and the Honor Code for the Bocconi Community. All participants gave informed consent. Identifying information was removed from all research materials, and materials are stored securely with access limited to the researcher and supervisor.

Analysis

Analytical Strategy

The interview material was analysed through an inductive and interpretative qualitative approach using the Gioia methodology. This methodology moves from user-centric first-order concepts into theory-centric second-order themes that are then merged into aggregate dimensions.

The analysed material is composed of a corpus of 11 audio-recorded semi-structured interviews supplemented with ethnographic fieldwork. Being in an active region of the hazelnut cluster during harvest season allowed accurate interpretation of the interviewee narratives and fulfilled the engagement with the research setting that the Gioia methodology instructs.

Coding Procedure

The coding followed three stages: first-order concepts, second-order themes, and aggregate dimensions. Open coding began by identifying recurring expressions, actions, and descriptions. This generated a large number of raw codes reflecting informants' own language, which were then consolidated through constant comparison.

The analysis moved from a large corpus of raw, informant-centred data toward a structured set of first-order concepts, second-order themes, and aggregate dimensions — preserving the integrity of informants' accounts throughout.

Open Coding & Axial Coding

Open coding identified recurring expressions, actions, and descriptions within the interview material, producing 1,989 raw codes that reflected informants' own language. Through constant comparison, duplicate and overlapping codes were merged, reducing the total to 1,595. After decoupling unrelated codes, the final analytical set comprised 1,458 codes.

Distillation of First-Order Concepts

From 1,458 codes, 127 first-order concepts were derived by grouping similar expressions under shared meanings. These concepts remained closely tied to informants' language while offering a clearer analytical structure.

Second-Order Themes & Aggregate Dimensions

First-order concepts were grouped into second-order themes by identifying underlying patterns across the data. The polyphonic design allowed comparison across actor groups — examining how producers, merchants, exporters, and governance actors described similar issues revealed relational patterns that informed the development of themes. From 127 first-order concepts, 12 second-order themes and 4 aggregate dimensions were derived.

Dimension Composition

The four aggregate dimensions capture different facets of the cluster's transformation. AD-3 (Strategic Intermediation) carries the highest share of coded material at 31%, followed by AD-1 (Agrarian Base) at 27%. Together, these two dimensions account for nearly 60% of all coded segments — a strong empirical signal about where the analytical gravity of the findings lies.

Interpreting the Matrix

Looking at the column sums, Governance actors contributed 538 segments (37%), Producers 512 (35%), Merchants 215 (15%), and Exporters 193 (13%). Structural Constraints is overwhelmingly Producer-coded (145 segments), while Institutional Voids draws most from Governance perspectives (102). Merchants as Informal Substitutes peaks in both the Producer and Merchant columns (97 and 76 respectively) — a dual visibility where merchants describe their own intermediary role while producers describe their dependence on it. Strategic Upgrading is Exporter-heavy (54 segments), consistent with vertical integration strategies concentrated at the export end of the chain.

This adds a relational layer to the frequency counts: it shows whose perspective is doing most of the constituting in each theme — a distinction that shapes the interpretive claims in the discussion.

Potential Biases & Limitations

Snowball sampling is susceptible to biases: the method hinges on the assumption of underlying social connections, and if networks are too cohesive, participants may gatekeep by withholding contacts. The technique relied on a limited number of initial "seeds," drilling into specific social clusters. The majority of producer interviews came from the villages of Fatsa district, generating a non-probability sample with limitations for generalisable inferences.

A further limitation is the uneven representation of actor categories, particularly the limited number of exporters (only one). Also, MNC representatives were absent from the primary data — limiting the capacity to evaluate how global corporate strategies structurally govern the cluster. The findings are intended to support analytic generalisation to broader debates on intermediaries and cluster transformation under neoliberal restructuring.
