From Informal Substitutes to Infomediaries

How Turkey's Black Sea hazelnut merchants survived the state's retreat after 1980 — and quietly became the market's new governing layer.

Every autumn, the price of the world's hazelnuts is decided in places most consumers will never see: small merchant shops in the towns of Ordu, Samsun and Giresun, where farmers arrive with sacks of nuts and leave with a number they had no part in setting. The global confectionery industry — above all Ferrero, the world's leading hazelnut buyer — sits at one end of this chain. At the other end sit millions of families with orchards inherited from their grandfathers. In between sits a figure that official statistics barely count and economic theory largely ignores: the merchant.

This is the story of how that figure changed. Before 1980, merchants were informal substitutes — stand-ins for banks and warehouses in places the state reached only thinly. Then Turkey's economy was liberalised, the great hazelnut cooperative Fiskobirlik withdrew, and the merchants did not disappear, as market reform might predict. They did the opposite. They became infomediaries: the actors who decide what everyone else gets to know about the market.

To see how, start with geography.

1
The Cluster

One crop, one coastline

The hazelnut is the world's second most cultivated nut after the almond, and no country comes close to Turkey in growing it. Of the 1,125,221 tonnes produced worldwide in 2023, Turkey grew 650,000 tonnes — 57.8%, more than every other country combined. Italy, the runner-up, managed 9.1%. And almost all of Turkey's share comes from a single narrow strip along the Black Sea coast.

Three provinces carry the cluster

Samsun, Ordu and Giresun alone account for 63.8% of Turkey's output — and 36.9% of the entire global supply. Steep slopes, a wet microclimate and a century of specialisation have made hazelnuts less a choice than a fate here: as one farmer put it, "it's a slope — nothing else can be done here."

Ordu: the hub

Ordu by itself grows nearly a third of Turkey's crop — about one sixth of the world's hazelnuts. The villages of its Fatsa district, where much of this study's fieldwork took place, supply roughly one tenth of global output on their own.

…in impossibly small pieces

That world-scale supply comes from over 7 million smallholder orchard parcels. The average holding is 1.29 hectares, divided into nearly six separate plots. This extreme fragmentation is the cluster's founding condition: someone has to gather all of this before it can be traded. Keep that in mind — it is the merchant's reason for existing.

There is no alternative… nothing can be done here. Look, it's a slope. If you dig it up, a flood would just wash it away.
— Producer, Ordu province (field interview, August 2025)
2
A Century in Five Acts

The cluster was not born a market. It was built — deliberately, institution by institution — and then, after 1980, unbuilt. The merchant's story only makes sense against that arc.

1925–1938 · The state builds a cluster

The abolition of the Ottoman tithe tax in 1925 opens the way for planned agriculture. Hazelnut zones are officially designated in Giresun and Artvin — partly, remarkably, to replace malaria-breeding rice paddies. In 1936 the First Hazelnut Council forges a pragmatic alliance between the young state and local merchants, who supply the liquidity and aggregation the state cannot. But the council's own records conclude that fragmented merchant activity cannot build a cohesive cluster — so the state builds institutions instead: an exporters' union, a research institute, the grain board TMO (1938), and, growing out of İş Bank's trading arm İş Limited, the cooperative that becomes Fiskobirlik.

1938–1980 · The Fiskobirlik order

For four decades Fiskobirlik is the cluster's anchor: buying from producers on behalf of the Treasury, storing a substantial share of the national crop, announcing the procurement price against which every other price is read. Producers recall queues of hazelnut sacks stretching kilometres outside its warehouses. Production climbs from a 1960s average of 123,600 tonnes to 256,800 tonnes in the 1970s. The merchant, in this era, is pushed to the margins — left mainly with one function the state never took over: informal credit.

1980 · The rupture

A balance-of-payments crisis, a military coup, and the January 24 Decisions launch Turkey's neoliberal turn. Agriculture's share of GDP falls 28%; its share of the labour force falls 33%. Price supports become populist instruments rather than structural stabilisers. The state begins a long withdrawal from the countryside — and from the hazelnut.

1994–2006 · The void opens

Fiskobirlik's Treasury-backed buying ends in 1994. The 1999 IMF-backed reform package replaces market intervention with Direct Income Support, and through the 2000s the cooperative's finances collapse — producers interviewed for this study recall delivering their crop and waiting a year to be paid. TMO steps partially back in from 2006, but the reference point is gone. In institutional terms, the cluster now has a hole at its centre.

2014–today · The new order

Ferrero acquires Oltan, Turkey's biggest exporter, in 2014 and becomes a direct player as Ferrero Türkiye — a buyer so dominant the Competition Authority (TRK) deems it a monopsony, later capping its purchases at 100,000 tonnes a year and mandating a 30,000-tonne minimum in autumn 2024 after finding price speculation. Between this global apex and 7 million fragmented plots, one layer now runs the local market: the merchants. How they got there is the heart of this story.

3
The Paradox

Growth under the old order

Through the state-led decades, production roughly doubles each generation — from an average of 123,600 tonnes in the 1960s to 256,800 tonnes in the 1970s. So far, so expected: supports, guaranteed purchases, stable prices.

Then the supports are pulled…

After 1980, subsidies shrink, the cooperative weakens, and rural Turkey empties out — the urban population share jumps from 25% in 1950 to 53.7% by 1985. Villages that once harvested collectively now depend on migrant crews, first from Georgia, later from Turkey's Southeast. Everything in the policy textbook says output should suffer.

…and production rises anyway

Instead, output climbs to a 442,000-tonne average in the 1990s, peaking at 580,000 tonnes in 1998. The market did not fall apart when the state left. Something else absorbed the coordinating functions — the credit, the storage, the price signals — that Fiskobirlik used to provide. That something was not a new institution. It was a very old one.

4
The Value Chain, Rewired

Before: the cooperative at the centre

In the old order, the chain had a visible spine. Producers sold mainly to Fiskobirlik, which stored the crop, announced the reference price, and dealt with exporters from a position of collective strength — in effect a bargaining agent for millions of smallholders inside the global market. Merchants operated at the edge, useful mostly when a farmer needed cash before payday.

The hollowing

Liberalisation removes the spine but not the need for one. Fragmented production still has to be aggregated; farmers still need credit and storage; somebody still has to translate world prices into a number at the village scale. The literature calls this an institutional void. On the ground it looked simpler: the queue outside Fiskobirlik's warehouse moved to the merchant's shop.

After: the merchant as the new hub

Today the chain runs producer → merchant → exporter → multinational, with Ferrero setting the tone from the top and roughly 800–1,000 merchants — no guild, no union, no register — running the bottom. Exporters openly describe merchants as indispensable: buying through them turns retail into wholesale, cuts transaction costs, and reaches villages no factory can. The nuts flow up. Credit flows down, via the Alivre forward contracts, from Ferrero's advances to exporters, to merchants, to farmers' pockets — as debt.

What is the difference between a trader and a bank, really?
— Producer, field interview, August 2025
5
The Merchant's Three Levers

Lever 1 · Credit

When banks would not lend to farmers, merchants would — cash today against hazelnuts at the next harvest. A wedding, a funeral, a bad year: the merchant's ledger was the village's only credit line. But a debt fixed in kilograms is merciless. One producer recalled the arithmetic of the pre-2000s: borrow 1,000 lira when hazelnuts trade at 2 lira, owe 500 kilos — plus 500 more added on top. "How are you supposed to recover from that?"

A failed harvest doesn't cancel the debt. It rolls it forward — and with it, the obligation to sell to that merchant, at his price.

Lever 2 · Storage

Hazelnut prices follow a brutal calendar: lowest at harvest in September, peaking in January–February. Whoever can wait captures the difference. Most farmers can't — no warehouse, no cash buffer. So they deposit their crop with the merchant under the trust-based emanet (consignment) system, or sell outright the week it's picked.

The merchant, holding both warehouse and cash reserves, waits on everyone's behalf — and keeps the spread. In 2018 the Competition Authority documented exactly this: storage control as the quiet engine of merchant bargaining power.

Lever 3 · Information — the decisive one

Credit and storage existed before 1980. What liberalisation added was the third lever. When Fiskobirlik's announced price disappeared, so did the one number everyone could check. Now the farmer learns the market through the merchant — who decides what to share, when, and how.

Producers told this study there is "no price information at all — zero." Exporters described merchants circulating inflated harvest estimates "purely commercially," to push prices down before buying. In a bad year, farmers who were told not to store their pest-affected crop sold at 20–30 lira; by April the same nuts fetched 180.

This is the move the thesis names: the merchant became an infomediary — an intermediary whose real commodity is knowing.

Have you ever seen a tick? It swells as it feeds. You think it will be satisfied and fall off, but it never does.
— A producer, describing merchants · August 2025
You're already seen as a thief… you're a merchant… you're labelled that way.
— A merchant, on the same reputation · August 2025
6
Who Can Wait

The seasonal squeeze

The price calendar is the cluster's most honest document. Prices bottom out in September, when the harvest floods in and every cash-strapped farmer must sell. They climb through winter as stocks clear, peak in January–February, and slide again from March–April as the next crop becomes predictable. The pattern repeats every single year — and every year, the same people sell at the bottom.

Each actor sells from a different point on the curve

Producers sell in September, at the trough, because debt and living costs leave no choice. Merchants, having aggregated cheaply, release stock toward the winter peak. Exporters price against their Alivre commitments. Position on this curve is not a matter of skill — it is a matter of liquidity and storage, which is to say, of power.

And above the curve sits Ferrero

Before the harvest even begins, April–May Alivre forward contracts fix volumes between exporters and multinational buyers. Then Ferrero delays announcing its purchase price — and the whole chain holds its breath. As one exporter put it: "TMO has declared its procurement price, yet we are still waiting for Ferrero. Not just us — everyone." The signal travels down the chain; the liquidity to act on it does not.

Postscript: what the grower is actually paid

The asymmetry shows up in the producer price itself. In 2023 an Italian grower received $4,070 per tonne; a Turkish grower $2,891 — barely above the world average, and 29% below Italy, despite Turkey supplying 57.8% of the world's crop and Italy 9.1%.

Volume without pricing power, in one comparison: the country that grows most of the world's hazelnuts does not command the world's best price for growing them.

Section 7 · The soft machineryWeddings, funerals, and other market infrastructure

None of this runs on contracts. It runs on trust — and that is precisely the point. Merchants attend their producers' weddings and funerals; they send food at religious holidays; many merchant–producer relationships are inherited, three generations deep. "Everyone has their own trusted merchant," as one trader put it, and merchants pride themselves on honouring a handshake even when the market drops before delivery.

Economic sociology has a name for this — embeddedness — and usually tells it as a warm story: social ties lubricate exchange, spread knowledge, hold communities together. The hazelnut cluster complicates that reading. Here the same ties that support the producer also bind him. The farmer who receives credit at his daughter's wedding cannot easily sell to a rival buyer in September. Trust is real, and it is also the mechanism that keeps the weaker party inside a relationship whose terms favour the stronger one. Embeddedness, this thesis argues, works as support and control at once — and the merchant's local rootedness is not a residue of tradition that markets will erode, but the very infrastructure on which the modern, globalised trade depends.

8
The Argument

Structural ossification: how informal networks hardened into governance

Put the pieces together and the thesis's central claim comes into view. The post-1980 transition did not remove intermediation, as market reform promised. It reorganised and hardened it.

Before the cluster's institutions existed, merchants informally supplied credit, inputs and storage — improvised substitutes in a landscape without banks. Fiskobirlik's rise pushed them to the margins; its collapse invited them back. But the merchants who returned were no longer stop-gaps. With the state's reference price gone, their generational knowledge of the market became the market's operating system. Informal practices that were once fluid and transactional ossified into the permanent governance structure of a sector worth billions.

The concept, in one paragraph

Infomediary (n.): an intermediary whose authority rests on controlling the flow of market information — interpreting, filtering, timing and releasing knowledge about prices, quality, supply and demand — on top of the older mediations of credit, storage and aggregation. The Turkish hazelnut merchant after 1980 is the type case: hazelnuts pass through his hands, but so does the information about them. The process that put him there is the structural ossification of informal networks — the hardening of once-flexible social practices into the cluster's de facto institutional infrastructure.

This cuts against two comfortable assumptions in the academic literature. Cluster theory, from Marshall to Porter, assumes information inside an industrial district flows freely — a shared asset of the "industrial atmosphere." Here, proximity produces not shared knowledge but managed knowledge: gatekeeping. And the global value chain literature, fixated on multinationals and exporters, misses the layer that makes the whole connection physically possible. Ferrero's dominance does not bypass the merchants — it needs them. No multinational can aggregate 7 million scattered parcels. Global power and local informality are not opposites in this story; they are partners.

9
Randıman

A cluster rebuilding its own rules

The story does not end in pure capture. In the space the state left, the cluster has begun reassembling governance from below — dispersed, technical, and organised around one number: randıman, the kernel yield. Producers quote theirs with pride ("I know my hazelnuts reach 60, even 62 percent"); merchants price by it; exporters run sampling, traceability and certification regimes against it, auditing orchards under ISO standards and multinational specifications. Agricultural chambers, legally strengthened after 2004, carry farmer demands to Ankara. A shared technical language now does some of the coordinating work Fiskobirlik once did.

But a shared language of value is not a shared distribution of power. Randıman tells everyone what a sack of hazelnuts is worth; it does not change who can wait for a better price, who holds the warehouse keys, or who hears Ferrero's number first. The cluster has developed a more technical vocabulary of governance without becoming more equal — which is, perhaps, the most policy-relevant finding of all.

10
What This Means for the Sector

Five takeaways for people who work on hazelnut policy

The void is informational before it is financial. What producers lost with Fiskobirlik was not only a buyer of last resort but a trusted, public reference price. Any intervention — TMO procurement included — that announces a price the market does not believe, or announces it late, leaves the merchant's number as the only real one. Interviewees were blunt: the harvest lasts about twenty days, and support that arrives outside that window is support on paper.

Regulating the top of the chain is not enough. TRK's quota and minimum-purchase rulings on Ferrero address monopsony power at the apex, but the mechanisms that move value away from producers — advance-payment debt, storage dependence, information asymmetry — operate at the village scale, below the reach of competition law as currently applied.

Merchants cannot simply be removed. They perform real, costly functions — aggregation, credit, storage — that no other actor currently provides at scale. Policy that ignores this (or moralises about "middlemen") will fail; policy that competes with it — accessible storage, timely credit, transparent price reporting at village level — changes the merchant's bargaining position without breaking the chain.

Structural constraints set the ceiling. An average holding of 1.29 hectares in six parcels, ageing orchards, ageing owners, and a labour force now dependent on migrant crews limit what any market fix can deliver. Governance actors interviewed here called openly for land-tenure restructuring; producers described a livelihood that "no longer supports itself."

Value capture, not volume, is the frontier. Turkey grows the crop but does not brand it, barely processes it, and — as one exporter noted — even the exchange is in Germany. Domestic processing, branding and the entry of new buyers were the interventions actors across the chain, including merchants themselves, said they wanted.

My system is the state, my name is villager, my surname is farmer, my title is urbanite. I am a citizen of the Republic of Türkiye, and I am searching for my identity.
— A governance actor's petition to the Ministry, recounted in interview · August 2025

Conclusion: the merchant was never the residue

Development economics long treated figures like the hazelnut merchant as transitional — folk institutions destined to dissolve as formal markets matured. The Turkish hazelnut cluster ran the experiment in reverse: the formal institutions dissolved, and the folk institution matured. Four decades after liberalisation, the merchant is not a leftover of the old economy. He is the load-bearing wall of the new one — the essential bridge between more than seven million smallholder parcels and the handful of multinationals that turn them into chocolate.

Whether that bridge is a service or a toll booth depends on where you stand on it. The producers, who called their merchant a tick and a bloodsucker, and the merchants, who called themselves self-sacrificing labourers carrying hazelnuts through the night, are both describing the same structure. This thesis's contribution is to name it: informal networks, ossified into governance; intermediaries, evolved into infomediaries. The state left the market. The market, it turns out, was the merchants all along.

How the research was done

The study is an interpretivist, abductive qualitative inquiry with a polyphonic design — deliberately interviewing every actor group so each could be read against the others. Fieldwork took place in August 2025, during harvest, across Samsun and Ordu provinces (including Fatsa, Ünye and Perşembe), reached through snowball sampling from three local seed contacts.

  • 22 semi-structured interviews with farmers, merchants, exporters and governance actors; 12 audio-recorded (9.55 hours; one recording withdrawn on consent grounds, leaving 11 in the coded corpus).
  • Archival sources: six original documents (1932–1938) from the Türkiye İş Bankası archives, tracing İş Limited's role in the founding of Fiskobirlik.
  • Official documents: chiefly the Turkish Competition Authority's 118-page Hazelnut Sector Report (2018), used to triangulate field accounts.
  • Analysis: the Gioia method — 1,989 raw codes distilled to 127 first-order concepts, 12 second-order themes, and 4 aggregate dimensions (erosion of the agrarian base; institutional voids and global hegemony; strategic intermediation; emergent quality governance). Coding in MAXQDA; interviews conducted under GDPR-compliant consent and full anonymisation.
Where it sits in the literature

The thesis works at the intersection of three literatures and pushes against each:

  • Cluster theory (Marshall, Porter): assumes information circulates as a shared asset within industrial districts — a symmetry bias. The hazelnut case shows proximity producing gatekeeping instead.
  • Economic sociology of embeddedness (Polanyi, Granovetter, Boschma and the French proximity school): treats social ties chiefly as coordination-enablers. Here the same ties stabilise asymmetry — embeddedness as support and control.
  • GVC / latecomer-cluster studies (Jucevicius & Grumadaite; Tekin Bülbül on hazelnuts): focus on MNCs, exporters and formal institutions. The thesis adds the missing layer below the exporter — the merchant network without which fragmented production could never reach the chain at all.

It also fills an empirical gap: the 1980s transformation is one of the least-studied periods in Turkish hazelnut scholarship, and merchant–producer relations are nearly absent from official reports.

What the study can and cannot claim

The findings support analytic generalisation, not statistical inference. Snowball sampling concentrated access in the Central Black Sea (notably Fatsa's villages — though these alone supply about a tenth of world output); the sample includes only one exporter and no MNC representatives; female voices are few. Accounts of sensitive topics — child labour above all — diverge sharply between governance actors (who minimise it) and producers (who describe it as routine, with children paid adult wages), and response bias cannot be excluded. The conclusions are strongest on local mediation and weakest on MNC internal decision-making and East–West regional variation, both flagged as future research.
