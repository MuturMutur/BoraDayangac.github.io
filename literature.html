Literature Review

Four literatures, one root system: reading the field like a hazel tree

Hazel does not grow a single trunk. It grows as a cluster of stems from a shared base — exactly how this review is built. Four bodies of literature rise separately, but they draw on the same underlying question: who carries knowledge through a market when the state withdraws?

Stem 3.1

Cluster Studies

To understand the structural dynamics of the hazelnut market, this stem establishes the theoretical baseline: how information is conceptualised within cluster theory, how its critics have answered, and what both leave unexplained about intermediaries.

3.1.1

Information in Cluster Theory

Marshall · Porter · Martins & Ling

Within cluster theory, information is specialised, localised knowledge that flows more effectively between collocated firms, suppliers and institutions. Marshall and Porter both treat the socialisation of information as the cluster's primary advantage — though for Marshall knowledge circulates through the industrial atmosphere, while Porter sees it as an institutionalised phenomenon.

Limitation — symmetry bias Both models assume information flows freely once the atmosphere is established. In the Turkish hazelnut sector this overlooks power asymmetry: merchants can hoard information on export prices and quality standards, turning a "shared asset" into a tool for market domination.
What the thesis takes When state-led knowledge hubs like Fiskobirlik withdrew after the 1980s, Porter's "institutionalised knowledge" was privatised. Whether the merchants' gatekeeping role is a product of liberalisation is the gap this thesis fills.
3.1.2

Relational Critiques of Cluster Theory

MacKinnon, Cumbers & Chapman · Jessop

Critics push cluster theory toward relational dynamics. MacKinnon et al. read clusters through uneven capitalist development; Jessop through the growing weight of social and institutional relations in the knowledge-based economy. Both converge on institutional thickness — the density of support structures within a cluster — as a source of competitive advantage.

Limitation — formalist bias Supporting institutions are conceptualised as organised entities: state agencies, associations, formal networks. This leaves little room for informal, relationally embedded actors — like merchants — performing institutional functions where formal structures are weak.
What the thesis takes A closer engagement with intermediaries as relational actors who carry knowledge and support in the absence of formal institutional frameworks.
3.1.3

Latecomer Clusters & Global Integration

Jucevicius & Grumadaite · Tekin Bülbül

The latecomer literature explains how developing economies join global markets: structural constraints — weak governance, low trust — are eased through global value chain (GVC) integration, multinational anchor firms, or local institutional entrepreneurs. Evidence from the Turkish hazelnut sector fits, with local constraints overcome through informal networks coordinated by dominant exporters.

Limitation — the missing middle Attention goes to exporters, MNCs and institutional entrepreneurs. The intermediation below the exporter level — how merchants shape information flows, bargaining relations and market access inside the cluster — remains insufficiently specified.
What the thesis takes The local mechanisms through which GVC integration is actually reproduced on the ground.
3.1.4

Agricultural Clusters

Dirven · Burger et al. · He et al. · Wardhana · Gálvez-Nogales

Agricultural clusters are variously framed as crop-specific agglomerations, integrated value chains, or input–output networks — "the entire range of input–output linkages in production and transactions" (Dirven). Studies differ in emphasis (firm cooperation, upgrading, agglomeration effects on farmer income) but converge on proximity as the defining feature.

Limitation — structure without governance The literature prioritises structural dimensions — value-chain linkages, firm networks — while saying little about how these relationships are governed, and about the intermediaries who mediate them and construct market-based social boundaries.
What the thesis takes The need for a framework capturing how market relationships are structured and strategically mobilised — which is why the review turns to economic sociology next.

Stem 3.2

Economic Sociology of Proximity & Embeddedness

Cluster theory explains why economic activity concentrates, but not how relations within clusters are structured and differentiated. This stem moves from the question of concentration to the question of social organisation — from Martin & Sunley's paradox of localisation in a global economy, through Morgan's socially embedded knowledge, to the asymmetries hidden inside "shared knowledge frames."

3.2.1

French School of Proximity Dynamics

Torre & Gilly · Boschma · Rallet & Torre

Proximity is multi-dimensional: cognitive, organisational, social, institutional — with geography as the physical basis of interaction. Boschma integrates the framework, treating geographical proximity as a facilitating condition that lowers barriers across the other dimensions. Contemporary scholarship agrees that co-location alone cannot explain innovation or knowledge exchange.

Limitation — formalist bias, again By categorising proximity into formal dimensions that solve coordination problems, the literature assumes proximity facilitates learning uniformly — overlooking how access to knowledge and coordination benefits may be unevenly distributed.
What the thesis takes Proximity examined as a means through which boundaries and asymmetries are produced in the post-liberalisation hazelnut cluster.
3.2.2

Social Embeddedness

Polanyi · Granovetter · Ramirez, Clarke & Klerkx · Uzzi

Economic action is situated within networks of social relations: inseparable from social context (Polanyi), embedded in ongoing networks (Granovetter). Recent work goes further — Ramirez et al. show intermediaries leveraging network positions to bridge fragmented actors and coordinate cluster development. Embeddedness shifts from a general condition of economic life to a resource mobilised by actors in advantageous positions.

Limitation — spatial fetishism Treating clusters as cohesive units (MacKinnon et al.) obscures internal asymmetries in access to information. Embeddedness is unevenly distributed — and can reinforce hierarchy within local networks.
What the thesis takes How ties of trust, reputation and kinship shape patterns of access — and structure economic activity — inside the cluster.

Stem 3.3

Economic Transition & Agriculture in Turkey

3.3

The Post-1980 Rupture

Aydın · Keyder & Yenal · Şafak-Çubukçu

All three accounts agree the post-1980 era is a fundamental rupture: the private market replaced state-led development as the formative power of the economy. Aydın reads the neoliberal shift as abandonment of the nationalist project (1930–1980) in favour of transnational agribusiness; Keyder & Yenal show globalisation sweeping away state-maintained networks, leaving small producers exposed to global price swings; Şafak-Çubukçu frames it as primitive accumulation, with the state governing through the market rather than intervening directly.

They differ on agency and geography: Aydın stresses marginalisation of small-scale production, while Keyder & Yenal find differentiated regional outcomes — fertile coastal strips generated new economic activity rather than uniform decline.

Limitation — the absent merchant Focused on macro-structural transformation (policy reform, privatisation), this literature documents the destruction of state institutions but rarely the micro-social level. By fixating on the "absence of the state," it misses the active presence of the merchant as a new, private regulator filling the void.
What the thesis takes A shift of focus to the local and relational dynamics — everyday practices, network relations, intermediary actors — through which governance was re-enacted after the state withdrew.

Stem 3.4

Turkish Hazelnut Studies

The case-specific stem: rich in descriptive detail, yet limited in its engagement with the relational and organisational dynamics that structure the cluster.

3.4.1

Policy-Oriented & Regional Development Studies

Şık · Cebecioğlu · Ay · Demirci

Şık examines sectoral competitiveness through EU Common Agricultural Policy harmonisation; Cebecioğlu the socio-economic effects of agricultural financing in Giresun; Ay the evolution of policy in Giresun since 1925; Demirci the structure of petty commodity production during the harvest in Fatsa, Ordu.

Limitation — top-down & thin samples Emphasis falls on policy frameworks rather than relational dynamics. Methodologically, many studies rely on small samples with restricted transparency (e.g. 8 farmers; undisclosed gender composition; interviewing relatives) — constraining what they can say about how producers, intermediaries, exporters and institutions actually interact.
3.4.2

Labour & Cultural Approaches

Baş & Ciğerci-Ulukan · Karadeniz et al. · Derelioğlu-Kaan

A strand attentive to underrepresented groups: the invisibility of women's labour in the sector (Baş & Ciğerci-Ulukan); child labour in the Giresun harvest of 2019, with an average working age of 15.9 and a minimum of 11 (Karadeniz et al.); and the intersection of hazelnut folklore and economic practice (Derelioğlu-Kaan), which widens the field beyond policy and production.

Limitation — the missing chain Labour conditions and cultural practices are examined in detail, but the mechanisms linking them to broader patterns of coordination and exchange in the local value chain remain underexplored.
3.4.3

Economic Organization & Agricultural Transformation

Başer & Bozoğlu · Yurtoğlu · Meral & Millan · Demiryürek & Ceyhan

Başer shows fragmented farms suffer lower profitability and higher emissions; Yurtoğlu uses archival data on the early republic (1923–1950) to show how state interventions like Fiskobirlik standardised production and built Turkey's global dominance; Meral & Millan and Demiryürek & Ceyhan analyse the drivers and economics of the organic transition.

Limitation — description over relation Substantial empirical insight into policy, labour, production and culture — but limited attention to how coordination, information flows and market access are structured within the cluster.
What the thesis takes The need to move beyond sectoral description and engage the relational dynamics underpinning the organisation of the hazelnut economy.

The Root System

What the Literature Cannot Yet Explain

Every stem above draws from the same soil. Digging beneath the four literatures, the review exposes two connected roots — the gaps this thesis grows from.

Root I · The Theoretical Gap

Intermediaries in the cluster–GVC connection

Cluster studies, economic sociology, and work on latecomer integration explain knowledge, proximity and global market connection — but do not sufficiently account for the role of intermediaries in linking clusters to global value chains through embedded, local relations. The literature remains limited in explaining how intermediaries, such as the merchants of the Turkish hazelnut cluster, fill structural holes within clusters.

Root II · The Empirical Gap

The understudied 1980s transformation

The case-specific literature on the Turkish hazelnut sector offers descriptive and historical material on policy, labour and production — but remains fragmented in its coverage of the sector's historical development. The 1980s neoliberal transformation, in particular, is understudied, and its implications for the organisation of the sector insufficiently examined.

Where the roots meet

Addressing the first gap through the second

By focusing on intermediaries in the post-liberalisation hazelnut cluster, the thesis contributes to debates on embeddedness and cluster–GVC connections while extending the empirical literature beyond descriptive accounts. Hence the research question at the base of the tree: How did the role of merchants as intermediaries evolve following the neoliberal restructuring of Turkey's economy in the 1980s?
